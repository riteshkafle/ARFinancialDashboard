# Loom Video Script (~2 minutes)

Tone: confident, conversational, like walking a colleague through your screen. Don't read
this word-for-word — know the beats and talk naturally.

---

**[0:00–0:15] — Hook & context**

> "Hey — I want to walk you through AR Copilot, a prototype I built that automates the
> accounts receivable follow-up process: chasing overdue invoices. This is the kind of thing
> finance teams do manually every week — checking who's late, deciding how urgent it is,
> writing a reminder, and following up — so I built a workflow that does all of that
> automatically, using n8n and OpenAI."

*(Screen: show the finance dashboard first — KPIs on screen as you talk)*

---

**[0:15–0:35] — The data**

> "It starts with a Google Sheet acting as the invoice ledger — 32 invoices across 20
> customers, a realistic mix of paid, pending, and overdue accounts, with amounts ranging
> from under a thousand dollars to eighty-nine thousand. Each row tracks the usual AR
> fields: due date, amount, status, and — this is the part the automation manages —
> days late, risk score, and whether a reminder's gone out."

*(Screen: scroll through the Google Sheet)*

---

**[0:35–1:10] — The workflow**

> "Here's the n8n workflow that runs this daily. It reads the sheet, filters down to
> unpaid invoices, and calculates how many days overdue each one is. Based on that, it
> assigns a risk tier — Low, Medium, High, or Escalate — using a simple, deterministic
> rule, not AI, because I wanted the risk decision to be predictable and auditable.
>
> Then it hands off to OpenAI, which generates the actual reminder email — and the prompt
> changes tone depending on the risk tier. A Low-risk invoice gets a warm, assume-good-faith
> nudge. An Escalate-tier invoice — thirty-plus days late — gets a firm but still
> professional final notice.
>
> That email goes out through Gmail, the sheet gets updated with the new reminder stage,
> and — this is the part I'm proudest of — if the risk is High or Escalate, it also posts
> an alert to a Slack channel so the finance team doesn't have to babysit the sheet."

*(Screen: walk the canvas left to right, pause briefly on the Code node and OpenAI node)*

---

**[1:10–1:35] — Proof it runs**

> "Let's look at a real execution — you can see every node ran successfully. Here's the
> actual email that went out, generated by the model, not a static template. And here's
> the Slack alert for one of the high-risk accounts — Massive Dynamic, eighty-nine
> thousand dollars, sixty-one days overdue, which is exactly the kind of account you don't
> want sitting unnoticed in a spreadsheet."

*(Screen: execution log → Gmail sent item → Slack message)*

---

**[1:35–1:55] — The dashboard**

> "And because raw invoice rows aren't useful to a CFO, the workflow also rolls everything
> up into a finance dashboard — total outstanding, total paid, overdue count, high-risk
> accounts, average days late, and total AR balance — recalculated on every run."

*(Screen: dashboard KPIs and charts)*

---

**[1:55–2:05] — Close**

> "That's AR Copilot — a small system, but it covers the full loop: detect, prioritize,
> generate, send, escalate, and report. Repo link and write-up are in the description.
> Thanks for watching."

---

## Recording notes

- Keep each screen visible for at least 3–4 seconds before cutting — don't whip-pan the
  cursor.
- If OpenAI rate-limits mid-recording, it's fine to show a previously-completed execution
  log instead of a live run — say so plainly rather than pretending it's live.
- Aim for 1:50–2:15 total; slightly under 2 minutes reads as tighter and more confident than
  running long.
